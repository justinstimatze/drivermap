# References

Key source literature for the mechanisms in this knowledge base. Each mechanism's
extraction cites its Wikipedia article; this lists the primary empirical sources.

## Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory

- Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A. (1979). Prospect theory: An analysis of decision under risk. *Econometrica, 47*(2), 263–291.
- Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1992). Advances in prospect theory: Cumulative representation of uncertainty. *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, 5*(4), 297–323.
- Arkes, H. R., & Blumer, C. (1985). The psychology of sunk cost. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 35*(1), 124–140.
- Ainslie, G. (1975). Specious reward: A behavioral theory of impulsiveness and impulse control. *Psychological Bulletin, 82*(4), 463–496. *(temporal discounting)*
- Trope, Y., & Liberman, N. (2010). Construal-level theory of psychological distance. *Psychological Review, 117*(2), 440–463. *(construal level theory)*

> Loss aversion and prospect theory are distinct mechanisms: loss aversion is the
> asymmetric weighting of losses vs. gains; prospect theory is the broader value/
> probability weighting framework. They share the same paradigm but activate differently
> depending on how a choice is framed.

## Person × Situation Interaction (Scoring Model)

The multiplicative scoring formula (`person_score * (1 + situation_score * k)`) reflects
the established interactionist tradition in personality psychology: traits are necessary
but not sufficient; situation enables what the person is disposed toward.

- Lewin, K. (1936). *Principles of Topological Psychology*. McGraw-Hill. *(B = f(P, E) — behavior is a function of person and environment)*
- Mischel, W., & Shoda, Y. (1995). A cognitive-affective system theory of personality: Reconceptualizing situations, dispositions, dynamics, and invariance in personality structure. *Psychological Review, 102*(2), 246–268. *(CAPS theory — situation features activate cognitive-affective units, not traits in isolation)*
- Funder, D. C. (2006). Towards a resolution of the personality triad: Persons, situations, and behaviors. *Journal of Research in Personality, 40*(1), 21–34. *(modern interactionist synthesis)*
